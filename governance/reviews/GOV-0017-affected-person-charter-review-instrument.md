# GOV-0017 — Affected-Person Charter Review Instrument

## 1. Purpose

This instrument supports structured review by people who may be affected
by MingOS.

It is designed to surface disagreement, discomfort, unintended power,
and missing rights—not to obtain favorable testimonials.

## 2. Participant groups

The review should include, where safe and appropriate:

- parents who have sought family support;
- adolescents or youth representatives;
- adults who have been described by another person;
- people who refused or disagreed with an interpretation;
- people affected by safety escalation;
- professional users;
- people who stopped using the service;
- people from different family, cultural, disability, and socioeconomic
  contexts.

No participant should be recruited through pressure from a professional
who controls their care or access.

## 3. Participation conditions

Before participation, explain:

- the purpose;
- what will be recorded;
- whether audio or video is used;
- how evidence is de-identified;
- who can access it;
- how long it is kept;
- voluntary participation;
- withdrawal limits after aggregation;
- whether compensation is provided;
- that service access does not depend on participation;
- that the review is not emergency or clinical care.

## 4. Interview structure

### A. Understanding and language

- What do you think the Charter is promising?
- Which words feel unclear, abstract, or too powerful?
- Does the text sound like it knows more about life than it can know?
- Which statement would you challenge?

### B. Being interpreted

- How would you feel if a parent, professional, or AI described you in
  the system?
- What would you need to see before trusting an interpretation?
- How should disagreement be shown?
- Which interpretations should expire?
- When should an interpretation stop affecting decisions?

### C. Children and third parties

- When should a child know that information about them is being
  recorded?
- What should a child be able to correct or keep private?
- When might parent access create harm?
- Who should review a dispute between parent and child?
- What would age-appropriate participation look like?

### D. Safety and escalation

- What would make a safety response feel protective?
- What would make it feel controlling or humiliating?
- Who should be responsible after the system flags risk?
- How should an incorrect risk decision be corrected?
- What information should or should not be shared?

### E. Data and memory

- What would you want to inspect, export, correct, or delete?
- Which records may need limited retention?
- How should the reason be explained?
- What would make cross-family analysis unacceptable?
- Would you accept de-identified use, and under what conditions?

### F. AI and human roles

- When do you need to know that content came from AI?
- When must a human review it?
- What should AI never decide?
- Could a supportive AI create dependency?
- What would healthy exit or reduced use look like?

### G. Commercial pressure

- Which messages would feel like anxiety is being used to sell?
- What would make paid support feel fair and transparent?
- Should risk information be separated from sales?
- How should the product behave when a user becomes more capable and
  needs it less?

### H. Violation and remedy

- How would you report a problem?
- Would you trust the project to investigate itself?
- What remedy would matter?
- When should a feature, professional, or partner be suspended?
- What should be made public?

## 5. Non-leading method

Reviewers must avoid:

- explaining why the Charter is good before asking;
- correcting participant criticism during the interview;
- treating confusion as lack of education;
- asking for endorsement;
- summarizing away emotionally difficult objections;
- recording identifiable case details not required for the review.

## 6. Distress and safety

If participation surfaces distress or risk:

- pause;
- remind the participant of the review boundary;
- follow the governed safety path;
- avoid turning review data into a general case record without a
  separate basis;
- document any necessary transition.

## 7. Analysis

Analysis should preserve:

- direct participant meaning;
- disagreement;
- group differences;
- uncertainty;
- minority positions;
- interviewer influence;
- missing participants;
- privacy limits.

A finding must not become representative merely because it is vivid.

## 8. Output

Use:

- `GOV-TPL-0002` for each restricted interview record;
- `GOV-TPL-0003` for each governed finding;
- public aggregate summaries only after `GOV-0018` review.

## 9. Minimum review threshold

Before Charter acceptance, the project should demonstrate:

- more than one affected group;
- youth or child-rights representation;
- at least one participant who disagreed with or exited a service;
- representation of third-party data concerns;
- documented changes and rejected recommendations;
- unresolved objections preserved.
